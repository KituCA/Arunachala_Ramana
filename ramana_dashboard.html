Arunachala
அருணாசலம் · Hill of Fire
The sacred red hill at Tiruvannamalai — said to be Shiva himself in the form of fire. Ramana believed it called him before he could understand the call. He would never leave its shadow.
The Turning Point — Floor of His Uncle's House, Madurai, 1896
He lay on the floor — not gently, but because he could not do otherwise — and stretched himself out, arms at his sides, like a man in a coffin. "I am dying," he thought. And then, beneath that thought, something else: "So?" He held that second thing carefully. And from somewhere beneath the floor of all his thoughts, a question surfaced — simple and devastating and ancient: Who is it, exactly, who is dying?
The Quiet Boy
In one of Tiruchuzhi's modest streets lived a boy so unremarkable in appearance that neighbours often forgot he had been sitting among them. He was not a bad boy. He was not a particularly good student. "His eyes were somewhere that had no location at all." His brothers argued over cricket, over mangoes, over who had the better marks. Venkataraman watched these arguments with gentle amusement, the way a river watches boats quarrel on its surface.
The Strange Sleep
When his cousins fell upon each other in a heap of limbs and snoring, nothing could wake them. But if you shook Venkataraman hard, he would not stir at all. "His eyes were closed but he was somewhere — or perhaps more precisely he was nowhere, which is a different thing entirely." Nobody thought this was remarkable. Nobody thought to look more carefully.
The Death & Awakening — The Pivotal Scene
Something moved through him — not from outside, but from somewhere beneath thought. It was not pain exactly. It was the absolute, unambiguous certainty that he was going to die. Not someday. Now.

He felt it in his chest first, then everywhere. His body seemed to turn to cold water. "He turned attention inward — toward the pure sense of I am, the raw flame of existence before it became entangled with a name or a face or a history." And then something opened. Something that could not die because it had never been born.

When he rose, the room looked the same. The books on the shelf had not rearranged themselves. The clock was still ticking. But he was different.
After the Awakening
He moved through his days as a man moves through a house he has lived in so long he no longer needs to think about the doors. Something in him had settled the only question that had ever mattered.

He still sat in the back of his classroom. He still lost arguments about cricket to his brothers. But inside, a compass had found north.
The Departure
He dressed quietly before the house woke. He left a note that said simply he had gone to find his father. "He meant something more specific than his family would know." He stepped out into the dark street and he walked — with the manner of a man walking toward something that had been waiting for him his whole life.

अरुणाचल
Arunachala · The Red Mountain · Shiva as Fire
"You called me, and so I came." Ramana later described the mountain not as a destination but as a summons. The name had seized his heart years before he understood why. He arrived, and simply never left — not because he stayed, but because leaving would have been absurd.
Core Teachings
"Who am I?"
The foundational inquiry. Not a mantra but a sword — to be used inwardly, tracing every thought back to its source, until only the witness remains and even that dissolves.
Self-enquiry (Atma Vichara)
Turn attention toward the sense of "I" before it becomes "I am this" or "I am that." Abide in that pure, prior sense. This is the direct path.
The Self is not achieved — it is uncovered
Liberation is not a future state. The Self is always already present. Sadhana is only the removal of the misconception that it is absent.
Silence as the highest teaching
His most profound transmissions happened in silence. Devotees reported their doubts dissolving simply by sitting in his presence, without a word spoken.
Selected Utterances
"The degree of freedom from unwanted thoughts and the degree of concentration on a single thought are the measures to gauge spiritual progress."
"There is no greater mystery than this — that being the reality we seek to gain reality."
"Your own Self-realisation is the greatest service you can render the world."
The Three Paths Recognised
Jnana
The path of knowledge and self-enquiry — his primary and recommended path. Turn inward, trace the "I" to its source.
Bhakti
Devotion to the divine as other. He accepted this too — surrender to God is another name for dissolving the ego.
Yoga
Concentration and stillness of mind. All paths, he said, lead to the same destination — the dissolution of the false self.
Written Works
01
Who Am I? (Nan Yar?)
His earliest and most direct text on self-enquiry. Written at the request of a devotee around 1902. The entire path distilled to its essence.
02
Upadesa Saram (The Essence of Instruction)
Thirty verses on the path to liberation. Originally written in Tamil verse; later translated into Sanskrit and other languages.
03
Ulladu Narpadu (Forty Verses on Reality)
A philosophical masterwork on the nature of Reality and the Self. Considered his most profound written teaching.
04
Aksharamanamalai (Marital Garland of Letters)
108 devotional verses addressed to Arunachala, composed spontaneously as he walked around the sacred hill. Deeply personal.
05
Talks with Sri Ramana Maharshi
Compiled by devotees from recorded conversations — perhaps the most accessible entry point into his teaching for modern readers.
Legacy & Influence
Ramana Maharshi taught without seeking students. He prescribed no elaborate rituals, demanded no renunciations, and charged nothing. He simply sat — and people came from across the world to sit near him.

Among those drawn to Arunachala: the philosopher Paul Brunton, who wrote A Search in Secret India and introduced Ramana to the Western world; Carl Jung, who studied his teachings from afar; and Somerset Maugham, who fictionalised him as the sage in The Razor's Edge.

His teaching of self-enquiry continues today through teachers in the Advaita Vedanta tradition, through Ramanasramam which remains active at the foot of Arunachala, and through millions of seekers worldwide who take up the single question he considered the only one worth asking.
The Ashram Today
Ramanasramam at Tiruvannamalai remains a living spiritual institution. The samadhi shrine, the meditation halls, the mountain circumambulation (girivalam) on full moon nights — all continue as they did in his lifetime.
